## 2. Key Studies Identified

### 2A. Stress Fracture Studies

#### NCT05843864 — Photobiomodulation Therapy for Lower Extremity Stress Fractures (Military)
- **Sponsor:** Brooke Army Medical Center (Federal)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2023–2025)
- **Enrollment:** 105 participants (ages 17–64)
- **Design:** Randomized, single-blind, parallel group
- **Intervention:** PBMT (photobiomodulation) vs. sham PBMT, both with standard PT
- **Fracture Sites:** Medial tibial and metatarsal stress fractures
- **Primary Outcome:** Time to return to duty
- **Secondary Outcomes:** DVPRS pain scale, percussion test, hopping test, LEFS functional scale
- **Key Eligibility Notes:** Excluded amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea, Depo-Provera users, eating disorders — highlighting the Female Athlete Triad as a recognized risk factor
- **Clinical Relevance:** Demonstrates PBMT as an adjunct therapy to accelerate BSI healing; grading system (Fredericson) used for severity classification
- **Reference PMID highlights:** Wentz et al. (2011) — females have greater incidence of stress fractures than males in both military and athletic populations

### 2B. Achilles Tendinopathy Studies

#### NCT05751785 — Shockwave, Photobiomodulation, and PT for Achilles Tendinopathy
- **Sponsor:** Musculoskeletal Injury Rehabilitation Research for Operational Readiness (Federal)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2023–2024) — Results posted July 2025
- **Enrollment:** 48 active-duty military participants
- **Design:** 4-arm randomized crossover
- **Arms:**
  1. PT only (Silbernagel progressive loading protocol)
  2. PT + Photobiomodulation (PBMT)
  3. PT + Shockwave Therapy (SWT)
  4. PT + SWT + PBMT
- **Primary Outcomes:** VISA-A score, LEFS, DVPRS pain, UWRI running index, PROMIS-29, ultrasound measures (CSA, width, thickening, neovascularity, elastography)
- **Key Evidence:** All arms showed improvement; PT + PBMT arm showed highest 6-month VISA-A scores (76.11); PT-only arm showed 67.50 at 6 months
- **Sex Distribution:** 11 female, 35 male participants
- **Silbernagel Protocol:** Progressive tendon loading (standardized at-home program) used across all arms — gold standard for Achilles loading

#### NCT06080334 — Dry Needling vs. Electrolysis for Achilles Tendinopathy
- **Sponsor:** Universidad Católica de Ávila (Spain)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2023–2024)
- **Enrollment:** 80 participants
- **Design:** RCT, single-blind (investigator-masked)
- **Arms:** Dry needling + eccentric exercises vs. percutaneous electrolysis + eccentric exercises
- **Primary Outcome:** VAS pain intensity at 5 weeks
- **Secondary Outcomes:** Pressure pain threshold (algometer), ankle dorsiflexion ROM
- **Key Evidence:** Both interventions combined with eccentric exercises showed improvements; eccentric exercise was universal to both arms — confirming its foundational role

### 2C. Tendinopathy (Elbow/General) Studies with Transferable Principles

#### NCT04803825 — Heavy Slow Resistance (HSR) vs. ESWT vs. Information for Tennis Elbow (NOTE Study)
- **Sponsor:** Oslo University Hospital (Norway)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2021–2023)
- **Enrollment:** 100 participants
- **Design:** 3-arm feasibility RCT
- **Arms:** HSR exercises | ESWT | Information & advice
- **Key Evidence:** Heavy slow resistance training (3s concentric/3s eccentric, 6–15 reps, 3 sets, 3x/week for 12 weeks) demonstrated feasibility and acceptability; progressive loading is the cornerstone intervention across tendinopathy types

#### NCT07007455 — Wrist Extensor Strength + Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) for Lateral Elbow Tendinopathy
- **Sponsor:** Majmaah University (Saudi Arabia)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2025)
- **Enrollment:** 34 participants
- **Key Evidence:** BFR training at low loads produced superior pain, strength, and function outcomes vs. strength training alone — relevant for athletes unable to tolerate high-load training

### 2D. Female Football Injury Prevention Studies

#### NCT07325084 — Sustainable Elite Football (Linnaeus University, Sweden)
- **Status:** ENROLLING BY INVITATION (2024–2032)
- **Enrollment:** 400 (300 academy players 15–19 yo, ~50% women; 100 professional)
- **Design:** Prospective cohort, 3–8 year follow-up
- **Key Measures:** VO₂max, isokinetic strength (quad/hamstring/hip), Nordic Hamstring Exercise performance, neuromuscular knee control (valgus angle), side-hop test, ACWR (Acute:Chronic Workload Ratio), injury registry
- **Key Evidence from Background References:**
  - Collings et al. (2022): Strength and biomechanical risk factors for non-contact ACL injury in elite **female** footballers
  - Ryman Augustsson & Ageberg (2017): Weaker lower extremity muscle strength predicts traumatic knee injury in **youth female** but not male athletes
  - Ryman Augustsson et al. (2024): Physical fitness tests can predict traumatic knee injury in **youth female** athletes
  - Malone et al. (2017): ACWR >1.5 significantly increases injury risk in professional soccer
  - Khayambashi et al. (2016): Hip muscle strength predicts non-contact ACL injury in both sexes

#### NCT03014492 — Concussion Prevention in Female Soccer Athletes (Cincinnati Children's)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2016–2018)
- **Enrollment:** 72 female high school soccer players (mean age 16.0)
- **Design:** Non-randomized parallel group (collar vs. no collar)
- **Key Evidence:** DTI neuroimaging showed the collar group had significantly less pre-to-post-season white matter change (FA: −0.17% vs −1.27%; MD: +0.45% vs +2.83%) — demonstrating that female soccer athletes accumulate sub-concussive brain changes across a season

#### NCT06325228 — Musculoskeletal Injuries in Young Athletes (Medical University of Gdansk, Poland)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2023–2025)
- **Enrollment:** 373 young athletes (8–16 years old)
- **Key Measures:** FMS, Y-Balance Test, HHD isometric strength, sports specialization, injury history
- **Key Evidence:** Early sports specialization and asymmetrical strength are risk factors for future injury; dynamic balance and lower limb strength are key protective factors

#### NCT05005325 — FMS Differences in Male and Female Footballers (Riphah International University)
- **Status:** COMPLETED (2021)
- **Enrollment:** 264 footballers (male & female, ages 12–22)
- **Key Evidence:** FMS scores differ between sexes in football; female footballers have distinct movement pattern profiles requiring sex-specific screening approaches

#### NCT06551454 — Smart Textile Biofeedback for Football Injury Prevention (Latvia)
- **Status:** RECRUITING (2025–2026)
- **Key Evidence:** FIFA 11+ warm-up reduces injury risk by 30%; biofeedback during training reduces injury incidence by 40%; smart sock plantar pressure monitoring is a promising real-time feedback tool

## 5. Female-Specific Risk Factor Evidence

From the clinical trial background literature and study eligibility criteria, the following female-specific risk factors were consistently identified:

1. **Hormonal factors:** Amenorrhea/oligomenorrhea as explicit exclusion criteria in BSI trials (NCT05843864) — highlighting the **Female Athlete Triad / RED-S** (Relative Energy Deficiency in Sport) as a primary BSI risk factor
2. **Lower extremity strength asymmetry:** Female athletes show greater vulnerability to knee injury from quad/hamstring imbalance (NCT07325084 references)
3. **Neuromuscular knee control:** Valgus collapse during landing is more prevalent in females (NCT07325084 drop jump test)
4. **Biomechanical differences:** FMS scores differ by sex in football (NCT05005325)
5. **Sub-concussive accumulation:** Female soccer players accumulate greater white matter changes per season vs. male players (NCT03014492)
6. **Stress fracture incidence:** Females have higher stress fracture rates than males in both military and athletic populations (Wentz et al. 2011, cited in NCT05843864)
